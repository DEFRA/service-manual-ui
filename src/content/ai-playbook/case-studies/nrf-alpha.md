# NRF alpha: highest-scoring assessment

Completed in January 2026. The team moved from a successful AI-enabled discovery phase into an alpha phase. They received one of the best service standards assessments ever recorded for a Defra alpha project.

## Overview

The NRF alpha phase ran from October 2025 to January 2026. This followed directly from the team's AI-enabled discovery phase. This continuity was significant.

The team did not need to learn how to use AI tools from scratch. They already understood what worked, what did not, and where the limitations lay. This experience translated into exceptional delivery quality.

At the alpha assessment, the team received one of the highest service standards scores ever recorded for a Defra alpha project.

The team's approach to AI matured during alpha. By this phase, they knew which tools to use for specific tasks, how to write effective prompts, and when human judgement was essential. They built their own prompt library, sharing successful prompts across the team and refining them over time.

## How AI supported the work

**Ideation** helped generate and explore potential solutions to design problems. AI provided a broader range of options to evaluate, supplementing human design thinking.

**Research synthesis** condensed findings and documentation. AI extracted key insights and identified patterns across multiple sources. The team could process more research without being overwhelmed.

**Document analysis** became routine. The team used AI to review existing documentation, extract relevant sections, and identify inconsistencies, particularly when understanding legacy systems.

**Prototyping** accelerated significantly. The team built working prototypes using AI to quickly test with users. This allowed more iteration within the same timeframe.

**Code exploration** helped understand existing codebases, identify integration points, and evaluate technical approaches as the team built out the alpha service.

**Prompt library development** became one of the team's most significant innovations. As team members discovered effective prompts, they added them to a shared collection, creating a cycle of continuous improvement.

## Tools used

- OpenAI ChatGPT
- Google Gemini
- Google NotebookLM
- Anthropic Claude
- Microsoft Copilot
- Cursor
- Obsidian

## Outcomes and impact

### Highest service standards assessment in Defra history

The team received one of the highest service standards assessment scores ever recorded for a Defra alpha project. Assessors commented on research quality, clarity of design decisions, thoroughness of technical exploration, and the team's ability to articulate their approach.

### Comprehensive prompt library built and shared

The team created their own prompt library covering common tasks like research synthesis, document analysis, code review, and user story writing. This reduced the learning curve and improved consistency across the team's work.

### Lean team produced comprehensive outputs

The team operated with a lean structure. Despite this, they explored more options and tested more prototypes than a traditional team without AI support.

### Mature and transparent AI use

The team's maturity showed in how they discussed their work. They were honest about where AI helped and where it did not, explaining validation processes and human oversight clearly.

## Challenges and what the team learned

**Writing effective prompts** took practice. The team addressed this through their prompt library, which provided templates and examples. Over time, prompt-writing skills improved across the team.

**The absence of a central prompt repository** in Defra meant the team built their own collection from scratch. Other teams across the department were likely learning the same lessons independently, duplicating effort.

**Validation remained critical** throughout. The team embedded validation steps into workflows rather than relying on ad-hoc checking. When AI produced summaries, team members reviewed them against source documents. When AI suggested code, developers reviewed it for correctness and security.

### Key lessons

**Adopt incrementally.** Start with low-risk use cases where errors are easy to spot. Build confidence gradually before expanding into higher-risk areas.

**Treat AI as an accelerator, not an authority.** AI helps work faster and explore more options, but human judgement must remain at the centre.

**Embed validation into workflows from the start.** Build checking into standard processes so validation happens automatically.

**Share learning actively.** Prompt sharing, discussing what worked and what did not, and collaborative problem-solving all accelerate team capability.

**Frame AI guidance as guardrails rather than rules.** AI can help identify risks and suggest approaches, supporting informed decision-making without imposing rigid constraints.
