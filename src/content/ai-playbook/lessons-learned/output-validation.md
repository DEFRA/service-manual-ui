# Always validate AI outputs

AI invented housing statistics for a discovery report. The numbers looked accurate at first glance, the formatting was excellent, but the data was fabricated.

## Overview

A discovery team was assessing the impact of a new service. The work involved collecting data from multiple sources. They needed to review recent news articles, government policy documents, and historical building records from planning councils. This is typical discovery work.

AI was being used across the project. One team member had used it to pull data from government websites. Another had used it to consolidate information from planning portals.

## What happened

A team member created a report about new houses built in a specific area of the UK. The report looked fantastic. When they showed it to the team, the team challenged the numbers.

The numbers had been generated by ChatGPT as part of the report. They were inaccurate.

The sophistication of the report hid the inaccuracy. When a report looks well-designed and professional, people assume the content is accurate too. That was not the case here.

The team caught it because they challenged the numbers.

## What this reveals

Large language models invent facts. They do not retrieve data. They generate text that looks plausible. If you ask for statistics, they will give you statistics. The statistics will look real. They will not be real.

Team members who see AI being used successfully for some tasks will assume it can be used successfully for all tasks. Without guidance on where the boundaries are, they cannot know when they have crossed them.

A well-formatted, professionally designed output creates trust. If the formatting is good, the content must be good too. This assumption is false but powerful.

## What the team learned

When bringing people onto teams that use AI tools, you cannot just show them what AI can do. You must explain what it cannot do and where the dangers are.

Generic guidance is not enough. Different roles face different risks. Developers face code quality risks. Researchers face data validation risks. Role-specific onboarding guides would be more effective than general training.

The guidance is "go slowly at first" rather than "this will revolutionise your workflow overnight". AI is a powerful and dangerous tool. People need to integrate it gradually into their work, not adopt it in one big hit.

Validation steps must be mandatory for any factual content. If AI produces numbers, statistics, or references, those must be validated before use. This should be part of the process, not an optional extra.

Training alone is not sufficient. Teams need clear processes that require validation. Without mandatory steps, people will skip validation when they are busy or when the output looks convincing.
